Teta Documentary

Web and Print Design / Photography and Marketing Material Design

Challenge: I joined the team of Teta, a short film detailing Alexandra Hidalgo‘s journey nursing her youngest son, just as filming was nearing an end. As it moved into post-production and submission to festivals, Teta needed a several materials designed to support it.

Team: Ten crew members in total, two of them designers

My Role: Photography, poster design, website design, photo editing, press kit design, dvd case design, social media banner design

Technologies Used: WordPress, CSS, DSLR camera, Adobe InDesign CC, Abode Illustrator CC, Adobe Photoshop CC

I began by working concurrently on a visual identity for the website and poster. I also designed the film's press kit and a DVD case and disc for distribution. As the film progressed into the festival circuit, I created a set of social media banners to keep Teta's followers informed.

Website

To design the film website, I had the opportunity to work with stills and create graphic elements that would be repeated across the site, press kit, and other materials. I collaborated with Alex to choose colors and fonts consistent with the rhetorical message of the film and worked within WordPress to make the site a reality. This meant everything from color correcting stills to display on the site to organizing images into a footer graphic following the progression of the film.

The process began by developing a simple outline for how the site should look. Once I had a basic idea of what we wanted out of the site, I created a wireframe.

After that, we set in on hashing out the design details of the site. Because my client had websites made for previous films, she knew what she wanted the information architecture to look like. Color schemes, fonts, and visual elements were my job.

Poster

On a brisk March day I shot the principal photography to use for the poster design. We had a plan, so we headed to a location that fit the idea for the poster.

After I reviewed and edited the photographs in Photoshop Camera Raw, we chose our top three to work with. From here, I worked together with my client to consider a tagline and a set of fonts to use within the design and tested them out in InDesign.

Once these details were settled on, I pulled the headline section into Illustrator and saved it as an asset in CC Libraries. I knew we would need posters at different dimensions and other future content that I could design more efficiently without having to resize and reorganize the headline every time. I did the same for other pieces of graphic content, such as the production company logo and the credit block. With everything in place for the next steps, the final poster could now be composed.
